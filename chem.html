1.1.4. DONE 4. Describe the effects of temperature and pressure on the volume of a gas

  • High temp -> high volume
  • High pressure -> low volume
  • And vice versa

1.1.5. DONE 5. Explain changes of state in terms of kinetic particle theory, including the interpretation of heating and cooling curves

  • Melting/boiling
    • Heat gives the particles kinetic energy
    • The KE weakens the inter-molecular forces So their volume expands
    • Once their KE is high enough They overcome their inter-molecular forces and change state
  • Condensing/freezing
    • They cool down losing KE
    • They can no longer overcome their inter-molecular forces
    • They change state
  • Evaporation Occurs at any temperature of a liquid, but only on the surface
    • In a liquid some particles have more energy than others
    • Those at the surface of the liquid, if they have enough energy, can become gaseous and leave

1.1.6. DONE 6. Explain, in terms of kinetic particle theory, the effects of temperature and pressure on the volume of a gas

  • Temperature on gases
    1. High temperature increases the KE of the gas particles(they move faster)
    2. They spread further apart from eachother
    3. The volume increases
    4. The opposite is also true
  • Pressure on gases
    1. When pressure increases, it pushes the particles closer together
    2. The volume is decreased
    3. The opposite is also true

2.6. 6) Giant covalent structures

2.6.1. DONE 1. Describe the giant covalent structures of graphine and diamond

  1. Graphite
    • Sheets of carbon atoms arranged in hexagons Each atom forms 3 covalent bonds
    • There aren’t any covalent bonds between layes So the layers are weakly held together and are free to move over each other
    • It has a high melting point
    • And is a good conductor as there are free electons(as one electron isn’t used for bonding)
  2. Diamond
    • A network of carbon atoms that each form four covalent bonds
    • The covalent bonds take a lot of energy to break so diamond has a high melting point
    • It has a rigid lattice structure which makes the diamond extremely hard
    • There are no free electrons so diamond does not conduct electricity

2.6.2. DONE 2. Relate the structures and bonding of graphite and diamond to their uses, limited to:

  1. Graphite as a lubricant and as an electrode
    1. Lubricant
      • Its layers are weakly held together
      • They slide over one another easily making them slippery Thus, a good lubricant
    2. Electrode
      • It is a good conductor of electricity
      • This is because it has free flowing electrons
  2. Diamond in cutting tools
    • The rigid lattice structure makes it very hard
    • So it is used to strengthen cutting tools

2.6.3. DONE 3. Describe the giant covalent structure of silicon(IV) oxide, \(SiO_2\)

  • It has a giant covalent structure like diamonds
  • The silicon atoms are positioned like carbon atoms in diamond, with an oxygen atom in between
  • It shares many of the same characteristics as diamond
    1. Very hard
    2. High melting/boiling point
    3. No free electons, thus, it is a bad conductor

5.3.1. TODO 1. Describe the preparation, separation and purification of soluble salts by reaction of an acid with:

  1. a) An alkali by titration
    1. Steps
      1. Measure out a volume of acid(with a pipette) into a conical flask
      2. Add a few drop if a suitable indicator(e.g. Methyl Orange) Universal indicator is not suitable as its change is too gradual
      3. Slowly add the alkali with a burette until you reach the end point End point: When the acid has been neutralised and indicator changes colour
      4. Record the volumes of acid and alkali used
      5. Steps 1-4 can be repeated multiple times with the same volume of acid to make sure the volume of alkali taken to neutralise is accurate
      6. Repeat the titration without the indicator, following the volume determined from the earlier titrations
      7. Evaporate the mixture in a heated evaporating dish
      8. Let the salt crystallise
      9. Filter then dry the salt
  2. b) Excess metal / c) Excess insoluble base / d) Excess insoluble carbonate
    1. Steps

      Pick an acid and base that will make the soluble salt you are looking for

      1. Heat the acid in a water bath
      2. Add the insoluble base until it all the acid has been reacted(base no longer dissolving)
      3. Filter out the insoluble base with filter paper
      4. Pour the filtrate into an evaporating dish
      5. Heat the evaporating dish until the solution has reached saturation
      6. Let the liquid evaporate and the salt will crystallise
      7. Filter off the salt and leave it to dry
    2. Safety precautions
      • Heat the acid under a fume hood
